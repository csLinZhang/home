Learning Quality-Aware Filters for No-Reference Image Quality Assessment

Lin Zhang, Zhongyi Gu, Xiaoxu Liu, Hongyu Li, and Jianwei Lu

School of Software Engineering, Tongji University, Shanghai, China

Introduction

This is the website for our paper "Learning Quality-Aware Filters for No-Reference Image Quality Assessment", submitted to IEEE Multimedia Magazine.

With the rapid development of the usage of digital imaging and communication technologies, there appears to be a great demand for fast and practical image quality assessment (IQA) algorithms that can predict an image¡¯s quality consistently with human perceptions. This paper addresses the problem of general-purpose no-reference image quality assessment (NR-IQA) with the goal of developing a model without any prior knowledge about non-distorted reference images and types of distortions. The key of our approach is to obtain effective image representations by means of learning quality-aware filters (QAF). Additionally, the random forest, instead of other regression models, is used to learn the mapping from the feature space to human subjective scores. Extensive experiments conducted on LIVE and CSIQ databases demonstrate that the proposed NR-IQA metric QAF can achieve better prediction performance than all the other state-of-the-art approaches in terms of both the prediction accuracy and the generalization capability.

Evaluation Results

The performance of QAF is validated in terms of its ability to predict the perceptual quality of a given image. Two widely-used IQA datasets are employed, LIVE and CSIQ. Five opinion-aware approaches, namely BIQI, BRISQUE, BLIINDS-II, DIIVINE, CORNIA and two opinion-unaware approaches, namely NIQE and QAC, were used for comparison.

Performances on single database

For training-based methods, we present their results under three partition settings: distorted images associated to 80%, 50% and 10% of the reference images are used for training and the remainders are used for testing. The partition was randomly conducted 1000 times and we report the median result. The performance of QAF and other NR-IQA methods is presented in Table 1 and Table 2.

Performances on cross-databases

We performed a more comprehensive performance evaluation by training on LIVE dataset and testing on CSIQ datasets. For the five opinion-aware NR-IQA methods, their quality prediction models trained on the entire LIVE dataset are provided by the original authors. Thus, we directly use them for testing on CSIQ. The experimental results are presented in Table 3. We can see that QAF performs significantly better than all the other state-of-the-art NR-IQA algorithms in this case, indicating that QAF has a better generalization capability than the other competitors.
